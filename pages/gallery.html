When Abroad, most Tourist enjoy a country's cuisine, nature, and landmarks. Japan is broken up into four main islands, Hokkaido, Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu. Let's look at the highlights of each Island!

Hokkaido

The Northern Island, a chilly place

Landmark: Goryokaku

Built in 1866, Goryokaku is a star-shaped fort in Hokkaido that's steeped in history and surrounded by more than a thousand cherry trees. In winter, it becomes a snow-dusted wonderland, while in spring, it's a sea of pink blossoms. A visit to the observation deck of the nearby Goryokaku Tower offers a stunning bird's-eye view of this architectural marvel.

Nature: Winters

Hokkaido's unique snowy weather creates a real-life winter wonderland, especially between December and February. The snowfall is not just abundant but also of high quality, making it a paradise for winter sports enthusiasts. Plus, the Sapporo Snow Festival showcases incredible ice sculptures, making the winter in Hokkaido a magical experience.

Cuisine: Curry

Hokkaido's soup curry, a rich, spicy, and hearty dish, showcases the Mongolian influence on the local cuisine. It features a variety of vegetables and a choice of meat, simmered in a flavorful curry soup, a nod to the island's agricultural richness. This dish, along with Genghis Khan, a grilled mutton delicacy, hints at the historic interactions with the Mongolian region.

Honshu

The metropolitan Island, heart of Japan

Landmark: Mt.Fuji

Mt. Fuji, Japan's tallest peak, is an iconic symbol of the country known for its near-perfect symmetrical shape. The mountain has inspired artists and poets for centuries and offers breathtaking views, especially at sunrise. Whether you're climbing to the top or viewing it from afar, it's a must-see in Japan.

Nature: Cherry Blossoms

Cherry blossoms, or "sakura," signal the arrival of spring in Japan, creating an enchanting backdrop of pink and white. They symbolize the ephemeral nature of life in Japanese culture and are celebrated with "hanami," traditional cherry blossom viewing parties. Cities across Japan become picture-perfect during this time, with blossoms adorning temples, parks, and riversides.

Cuisine: Sushi

Nigiri sushi, a classic Japanese dish, involves a slice of raw fish or seafood placed atop a mound of vinegared rice. This simple yet elegant dish showcases the freshness and quality of the ingredients, a testament to Japan's culinary philosophy. Whether enjoyed at a high-end sushi bar or a local market, it's a taste of authentic Japan.

Shikoku

Get off the beaten path with the smallest major island!

Landmark: Matsuyama

Matsuyama Castle, located on Shikoku Island, is one of the few original castles remaining in Japan. With its impressive stone walls and strategic hilltop location, it offers panoramic views of Matsuyama city and the Seto Inland Sea. Walking through the castle's historical interiors and structures is like stepping back in time.

Nature: Kazura Bridge

Kazura Bridge, a vine suspension bridge located in the Iya Valley of Shikoku, offers a thrilling and scenic adventure. This 800 year old, 45-meter long bridge sways over the turquoise Iya River, providing breathtaking views. It's a testament to ancient engineering techniques and a symbol of the region's rich history.

Cuisine: Wagyu Beef

Wagyu beef is world-renowned for its marbling, tenderness, and rich, buttery flavor. Originating in Japan, this luxurious meat is a culinary delight, whether grilled as steak, served as sashimi, or used in a hotpot. Eating wagyu beef is more than a meal; it's an indulgent experience.

Kyushu

The tropical island of the South

Landmark: Fukuoka Tower

Fukuoka Tower, the tallest seaside tower in Japan, stands as a symbol of Fukuoka city on Kyushu Island. Its mirrored exterior reflects the city and sky, while the observation deck offers panoramic views of Fukuoka and Hakata Bay. At night, the tower's illuminations make it a mesmerizing sight.

Nature: Kawachi Garden

Kawachi Wisteria Garden in Kitakyushu is a dreamlike spectacle that feels like walking through a painting. In the springtime, tunnels of cascading wisteria in various colors, from white to deep violet, create a mesmerizing display that's a feast for the eyes. This enchanting place is a bucket-list destination for flower lovers and photographers alike.

Cuisine: Udon

Kyushu style Udon is a delight that's not to be missed when visiting Japan's southernmost island. These thick, chewy noodles are often served in a rich pork broth, a nod to the island's famous "tonkotsu" ramen. Topped with local ingredients like green onions and slices of tender pork, a bowl of Kyushu Udon is a soul-satisfying meal that represents the region's flavors.
